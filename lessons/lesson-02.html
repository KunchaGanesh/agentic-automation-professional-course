Identifying Use Cases for Agentic Automation

Identifying High-Impact Agentic Automation Opportunities

What Is Agentic Automation?

Agentic automation refers to automation that goes beyond executing predefined, rule-based steps. An agentic system can perceive its environment, reason about the situation, make decisions, and take actions — adapting dynamically to inputs and outcomes rather than following a fixed script. Unlike traditional RPA, which follows deterministic paths, agentic automation handles variability, ambiguity, and judgment-intensive tasks by combining AI reasoning with automation execution.

In the UiPath platform, agentic automation is delivered through AI agents that can converse, decide, call tools, query knowledge bases, and orchestrate other automations — all in response to dynamic, real-world inputs.

Why Process Selection Matters

Not every process is a strong candidate for agentic automation. Applying agentic solutions to the wrong processes wastes investment and underdelivers on value. Applying them to the right processes — those that genuinely require reasoning, adaptability, and judgment — produces transformational outcomes that traditional automation cannot achieve.

The discipline of identifying high-impact agentic automation opportunities is therefore one of the most strategically important skills in an automation programme. It determines where effort is focused, where budget is allocated, and ultimately whether the automation strategy delivers meaningful business value.

Not every business process is a good fit for agentic automation. Unlike traditional RPA, which excels at repetitive, rule-based tasks, agentic automation thrives in environments where complexity, variability, and decision-making are required. To maximize impact, it's essential to understand what makes a process suitable for agentic automation and to apply clear criteria when evaluating opportunities.

Key Characteristics for Agentic Automation

Agentic automation is most effective when applied to processes that go beyond simple, repetitive tasks. Expand each section below to learn what features to look for when identifying agentic opportunities.

Complex Decision-Making

Processes that require nuanced judgment, adaptive logic, or the ability to weigh multiple factors are strong candidates for agentic automation. Agents can handle ambiguity and make informed decisions based on changing inputs.

Multi-Step, Variable Workflows

When a workflow involves many steps and conditional paths, agents can dynamically adapt to different scenarios, ensuring the right actions are taken at each stage.

Data Interpretation & Insight Generation

Agentic automation excels at analyzing data, extracting insights, and making recommendations—far beyond simply moving or copying information.

Natural Language Interaction

Processes that require understanding or generating human language, such as responding to emails or interpreting unstructured text, are ideal for agentic automation.

Cross-System Integration

Agents can orchestrate workflows that span multiple platforms, tools, or data sources, breaking down silos and enabling seamless operations.

Adaptive & Learning-Based

When processes evolve over time or require learning from new data, agentic automation can adapt and improve its performance continuously.

Contextual & Knowledge-Based

Processes that depend on business knowledge, context, or compliance requirements benefit from agents that can access and apply relevant information in real time.

Is My Process Right for Agentic Automation?

Agentic automation excels when inputs are unstructured, multimodal, or require contextual understanding—where decisions must happen at runtime based on partial or evolving context. Agents are ideal for processes that require judgment, flexibility, and contextual awareness in dynamic, non-deterministic environments—especially unstructured, exception-heavy workflows where conditions and outcomes vary. Consider agents if your process involves ambiguous tasks (like diagnosing a support ticket or researching a market), highly variable inputs or paths, or situations where learning from each run adds value. However, workflows excel in high-volume, repeatable processes with clear business rules and predictable outcomes—so assess your process structure before deciding.

Agentic Automation Across Industries

Agentic automation is transforming industries such as banking, healthcare, insurance, manufacturing, and telecom by enabling AI-powered agents to tackle complex, high-value tasks. Examples include loan eligibility validation in banking, medical record summarization in healthcare, and fraud detection in insurance. This cross-industry adoption demonstrates the versatility and impact of agentic automation in solving real-world challenges.

Agentic Automation in Action: Real-World Stories

Expand each story to understand how agentic automation is structured and measured for success in different industries.

Flight Disruption Manager (Transportation)

As a Flight Disruption Management Agent, the agent operates during flight delays and cancellations to proactively manage passenger itineraries. It automatically rebooks flights, notifies passengers, and offers compensation options, interacting via mobile notifications and portals. Success is measured by reduced complaints, improved on-time metrics, and increased customer retention.

KYC Research Agent (Banking/Finance)

This agent automates customer due diligence by gathering information from multiple systems, performing risk assessments, and ensuring compliance with regulations. It adapts to new requirements and integrates data from diverse sources to provide comprehensive research and recommendations.

Medical Record Summarization Agent (Healthcare)

The agent interprets unstructured medical records, summarizes key findings, and ensures compliance with healthcare standards. It supports clinicians by providing quick, accurate insights and adapting to evolving documentation practices.

Promo/Offer Qualification Agent (Telecom)

This agent evaluates customer eligibility for promotions and offers, using dynamic, multi-step logic. It adapts to changing criteria and integrates with various systems to deliver timely, personalized recommendations.
