SLAs, Escalation Rules & Expiry Timers

Ensuring Timely Automation: The Power of

SLAs and Escalation in UiPath Maestro

Time management is a cornerstone of successful business automation. In orchestrated workflows, delays and bottlenecks can undermine efficiency and erode accountability. Service Level Agreements (SLAs) and escalation rules are essential tools in UiPath Maestro, ensuring that every task and process step is completed within defined deadlines. By leveraging these features, you can prevent stalled processes, drive timely action, and maintain a high standard of operational reliability.

This lesson will guide you through the essential skills needed to manage Service Level Agreements (SLAs) and escalation processes in Maestro. By the end, you'll be able to configure SLAs, set up escalation rules, manage task deadlines, and apply your knowledge to real-world scenarios.

What you’ll learn

  1. Define and configure SLAs at both case and task levels in Maestro.
  2. Set up escalation rules to automate notifications, reassignments, and management alerts.
  3. Apply expiry timers to individual tasks for precise deadline management.
  4. Analyze real-world workflow examples of escalation and expiry in action.

Understanding SLAs in Maestro: Why Deadlines Matter

Service Level Agreements (SLAs) are formal definitions of how long a step or an entire case should take to complete within a workflow. Without SLAs, tasks can linger indefinitely, leading to process delays and missed business objectives. Maestro addresses this risk by providing structured SLA management at both the case and stage levels, allowing you to set clear expectations and enforce accountability throughout your automation processes.

SLAs in Maestro: Essentials

Review the key concepts of Service Level Agreements (SLAs) as they apply in Maestro. Understanding SLAs helps ensure timely completion of tasks and prevents process delays.

What is a Service Level Agreement (SLA)?

A defined time within which a step or case must be completed.

What happens if no SLA is set on a task?

The task can stall indefinitely, risking process delays.

At what levels can SLAs be configured in Maestro?

At the case level and the stage level, independently.

Types and Lifecycle of SLAs in Maestro

Service Level Agreements (SLAs) in Maestro help ensure timely completion of cases and stages by defining deadlines, monitoring progress, and triggering escalation actions when necessary. Below, explore the types of SLAs and how their lifecycle is managed within Maestro Case workflows.

Case-Level and Stage-Level SLAs

  • In case-style workflows managed by Maestro Case, SLAs are configured in the Case Plan
  • Designer.
  • SLA time can be measured in business hours/days (excluding weekends and holidays) or calendar time. Each case and each stage within a case can have its own SLA defined independently. SLA states progress through three stages: On-Track (the case/stage is proceeding within the allotted time), At-Risk (the deadline is approaching; a configurable warning threshold has been crossed), and Breached (the SLA deadline has passed without completion).

Escalation rules define automatic actions when an SLA reaches At-Risk or Breached state: notify specified users or groups via alerts, reassign the case or stage to a different owner, or flag the case for management review. SLA timers can be paused and resumed — for example, pausing while waiting for an external document, then resuming when it arrives. Example: A complaint handling case has a 5-day SLA. The SLA is set to At-Risk after 3 days (60%) and Breached after 5 days. When At-Risk, an escalation rule notifies the team lead. When Breached, the case is automatically reassigned to a senior manager and flagged in the dashboard.

SLA States and Escalation Actions

SLA states progress through three stages: On-Track (the case or stage is within the allotted time), At-Risk (the deadline is approaching and a warning threshold has been crossed), and Breached (the deadline has passed without completion). Escalation rules define what happens at each state. At-Risk can trigger alerts to specified users or groups. Breached can result in automatic reassignment to a different owner or flagging the case for management review. These actions ensure that no critical task or process step is left unattended.

Task-Level Expiry Timers: Fine-Grained Deadline Control

While SLAs govern overall process or stage deadlines, task-level expiry timers (Task Timers) provide precise control over individual Action Center tasks. Task Timers are essential for ensuring that each human-in-the-loop activity is completed promptly, enabling immediate escalation or automated action if deadlines are missed. This finegrained timing helps maintain momentum and accountability at every step of your workflow.

Key Configuration Options for Task Timers

Task Timers offer flexible configuration options to help you manage task deadlines and automate actions based on timing. Below are the main options you can set for Task Timers:

Start and End Criteria

The countdown for a Task Timer can begin when the task is created or when it is assigned. The timer stops when the task is assigned or completed, depending on your configuration. This flexibility allows you to tailor timing to the specific needs of each task. For example, you might start the timer when a task is assigned to ensure the assignee acts within a set period, or start at creation to track overall task age.

Timer Duration and Expressions

Timer Duration is expressed in days, hours, and minutes (e.g., 2d 04h 00m). You can also use expressions for dynamic durations, adapting deadlines based on process variables or business rules. This allows you to set different expiry times for different types of tasks or users, increasing the flexibility and precision of your workflow timing.

Outcome on Expiry

When a Task Timer expires, several automatic actions are available: Alert Assignee (sends a notification), Reassign to User (moves the task to a specified user or group), Unassign (removes the current assignee so the task re-enters the pool), or Auto Complete (closes the task with a default outcome). Users in Action Center see timer status as Due (approaching deadline), Due soon (within warning window), or Overdue (past deadline). Supervisors can still manually reassign, unassign, edit, or remove tasks as needed.
