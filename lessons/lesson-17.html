Handling Failures and Monitoring Asynchronous Workflows

Why Robust Error Handling and Monitoring

Matter in Async E-Commerce

Even the most carefully designed asynchronous systems can encounter unexpected issues—orders may get stuck, callbacks might be missed, or failures could go undetected for hours. In high-volume e-commerce environments, these silent failures can erode customer trust and disrupt business operations. That’s why proactive error handling and continuous monitoring are not just technical best practices—they’re essential for delivering reliable, resilient order processing at scale.

In this lesson, you will gain essential skills for working with asynchronous agents and workflows. The following objectives outline what you will learn:

What you’ll learn

  1. Identify common async agent failures
  2. Apply error mitigation strategies
  3. Monitor key async workflow metrics
  4. Interpret job logs for troubleshooting
  5. Implement best practices for reliability

Common Failure Scenarios in Asynchronous Agent Execution

Explore the most common failure scenarios in asynchronous agent execution and how each one impacts your e-commerce workflows.

Job Timeout

A job timeout occurs when an agent takes longer than the maximum allowed duration and is terminated by the system. This can happen if the agent calls a slow external service or processes more data than expected. Detection involves monitoring job status for 'timed_out' and receiving timeout notifications. Handling includes logging the timeout with relevant IDs, routing the order to manual review, and alerting operations if timeouts spike.

Callback Delivery Failure

This failure happens when the agent completes successfully, but the callback cannot be delivered—perhaps the webhook endpoint is down or there’s a network error. Detection relies on orchestration logs showing failed deliveries or non-200 responses. Mitigation involves retry logic, durable result storage, and using a DLQ for undeliverable events.

Duplicate Callback

Duplicate callbacks occur when the same completion event arrives more than once, often due to network retries or at-least-once delivery guarantees. The risk is duplicate processing. Prevent this by implementing idempotency: check if the Job ID has already been handled before acting on a callback.

Invalid Output

Sometimes, an agent completes but returns output that is malformed, missing required fields, or logically incorrect. Detection requires validating output structure in the callback handler. If invalid, route to a fallback process and log the raw output for investigation.

Async failures are often silent and can cascade if not detected early—robust handling and monitoring are nonnegotiable for operational excellence.

Understanding Dead-Letter Queues (DLQ) in Workflow Protection

A Dead-Letter Queue (DLQ) acts as a safety net for your workflows, capturing events that fail to process after multiple attempts. This ensures that undetected failures do not go unnoticed and can be addressed systematically.

  1. 1
    Callback Delivery Fails
    After several retry attempts, a callback still cannot be delivered to its endpoint. This could be due to persistent network issues or a misconfigured URL.
  2. 2
    Event Moved to DLQ
    The undelivered event is automatically moved to the Dead-Letter Queue, ensuring it is not lost and can be reviewed later.
  3. 3
    Operations Team Monitors DLQ
    The operations team regularly checks the DLQ for new entries, investigating the root causes of undelivered events.
  4. 4
    Replay After Fix
    Once the underlying issue is resolved, items in the DLQ can be replayed to ensure successful processing and system recovery.

DLQ Ensures Workflow Reliability

By capturing and managing undelivered events, a Dead-Letter Queue helps maintain workflow reliability, allowing teams to address failures and recover system operations efficiently.

Best Practices for Error Handling in Async Workflows

Explore essential strategies to ensure robust error handling and detection in asynchronous workflows. Click through each tab to learn how to maintain reliability and data integrity in distributed systems.

Idempotency ensures that each callback is processed only once, even if it is received multiple times due to retries or network issues. By storing processed Job IDs, your system can safely ignore duplicates and prevent double updates or notifications. Building idempotency into your callback handlers is critical for maintaining data integrity and a seamless customer experience. This practice is especially important in distributed systems where duplicate events are common.

Always define a fallback path for your async workflows. If an agent fails or a callback never arrives, your system should have a clear process for handling the exception—such as routing the order to manual review or triggering an alert. Without a fallback, failed jobs can leave orders unresolved and customers waiting indefinitely. A well-designed fallback ensures business continuity even when things go wrong.

Set explicit timeout values for your async jobs rather than relying on system defaults. This allows you to control how long the system waits before considering a job as failed or stuck. Graceful timeout handling includes logging the event, notifying operations, and moving affected orders to a queue for further action. This prevents silent failures and backlog buildup.

Make monitoring the Dead-Letter Queue a daily operational habit. A growing DLQ is an early warning sign of systemic issues in callback delivery or agent execution. Regular DLQ review helps you catch and resolve failures before they impact customers or business metrics. It’s a key part of maintaining healthy async workflows.

Critical Metrics and Insights for Monitoring Async Workflows

Effectively monitoring your asynchronous workflows requires tracking key metrics and analyzing job logs. Below are the most important metrics and insights to ensure

workflow health and reliability.

Job Submission and Completion Rates

Tracking how many jobs are submitted and completed per minute or hour reveals the overall health and throughput of your async workflows. Sudden drops or spikes can indicate bottlenecks, failures, or unexpected surges in demand that require investigation.

Timeout and Callback Success Rates

Monitoring the rate of job timeouts and callback delivery success helps you spot early signs of trouble. A timeout rate above 0.5% or callback success below 99% signals systemic issues. These metrics allow you to proactively address problems before they escalate and impact customers.

Dead-Letter Queue Depth

The DLQ should remain near zero. A growing DLQ means unresolved failures are accumulating, pointing to persistent delivery or processing issues. Regularly reviewing DLQ entries helps you prioritize fixes and maintain workflow reliability.

End-to-End Latency

Measuring the time from order placement to completed callback handling gives you a true picture of customer experience and system performance. Increasing latency can indicate hidden slowdowns or failures in the async pipeline.

Interpreting Job Logs

  • Job logs are your primary source of truth when diagnosing async failures. Look for job_id, status, timestamps, error codes, and tool call results.
  • If a callback is missing, check if the job status is Completed (issue in delivery) or Failed/Timed
  • Out (issue in execution) to quickly pinpoint the root cause.
