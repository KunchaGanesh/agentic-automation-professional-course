Knowledge Indexing & Retrieval with Context Grounding

Bridging Knowledge Gaps: Empowering AI with Organization-Specific Information

While large language models have broad general knowledge, they often lack the specific details, policies, and procedures unique to your organization. Context Grounding bridges this gap by indexing your internal documents—whether stored in Orchestrator buckets, SharePoint, Google Drive, or other repositories—and making them available to AI agents and automation. This approach provides evidence from your own data to influence LLM generations, making predictions more tailored to your use cases rather than based on generic training data. The result: answers that are accurate, relevant, and trustworthy because they are grounded in your organization's knowledge rather than inference alone. By reducing hallucination risk through references to ground truth data, Context Grounding enables AI-powered automation that your business can rely on.

This lesson will guide you through essential concepts and practical skills related to knowledge indexing and retrieval using advanced architectures and tools.

What you’ll learn

  1. Describe the three-stage Context Grounding architecture for knowledge indexing and retrieval.
  2. Create and maintain knowledge indexes in Orchestrator.
  3. Compare Semantic Search and DeepRAG retrieval methods.
  4. Configure agents and Autopilot to use knowledge indexes.
  5. Monitor, tune, and troubleshoot knowledge retrieval for accuracy.

Context Grounding Architecture: The ThreeStage Process

Context Grounding transforms your organization’s documents into searchable knowledge that AI agents can use to answer questions accurately. The process consists of three main stages: Ingestion, Indexing, and Retrieval. Ingestion extracts and processes content from your documents, Indexing stores this content in a way optimized for fast and relevant search, and Retrieval ensures that AI agents access the most relevant information when responding to user queries. Understanding this architecture is key to delivering reliable, organization-specific answers.

Making Organizational Knowledge AI-Accessible: Overview

This lesson explains the step-by-step process of transforming your organization's knowledge into a format that is accessible and usable by AI systems. Follow the stages below to understand how information is ingested, indexed, and retrieved for optimal AI performance.

  1. 1
    Ingestion
    Business data is processed and converted into representative embeddings using UiPathmanaged embedding models.
  2. 2
    Indexing
    The generated embeddings are stored in a UiPath-managed vector database and organized into indexes for efficient search and retrieval.
  3. 3
    Retrieval
    When a user submits a query, Context Grounding searches the indexed data using extraction, chunking, retrieval, and re-ranking techniques. The most relevant information is identified through similarity search and provided as contextual evidence to the LLM before response generation.

Summary: Ensuring Relevant, Up-to-Date Answers

This three-stage pipeline guarantees that your organization's most relevant and current information is used to answer questions, rather than relying solely on generic training data. This approach enhances the accuracy and reliability of AI-generated responses.

Only derived text and vector embeddings are stored; the original file is not kept.

Via the Orchestrator Indexes page or the Update Context Grounding Index activity.

It ensures answers are based on current, organization-specific documents, not just generic LLM training data.

HR Policies and Employee Handbooks

Context Grounding in Action: HR Policies & Employee Handbooks

One of the most practical applications of Context Grounding is indexing an organization's HR policies and employee handbooks. Instead of employees asking HR staff basic policy questions — or getting generic answers from a general-purpose AI — a Context Grounding index built from your HR documents enables agents and Autopilot to answer with accurate, companyspecific information.

What Gets Indexed

  • Annual leave and sick leave entitlements
  • Parental leave and flexible working policies
  • Expense reimbursement limits and procedures
  • Code of conduct and disciplinary procedures
  • Benefits, health insurance, and pension information
  • Onboarding checklists and probation guidelines

How It Works in Practice

An employee opens Autopilot for Everyone and asks: "How many days of annual leave do I get in my first year?" Instead of a generic answer, Autopilot queries the HR Policies index, retrieves the exact section from the employee handbook, and responds with the correct entitlement — with a citation pointing to the source document.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for direct factual questions: "What is the notice period for resignation?" Use DeepRAG for complex questions requiring synthesis: "Can I combine parental leave with unpaid leave, and how does this affect my pension contributions?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Name your index descriptively and with versioning: HR-Policies-2025-Q3 rather than just HR or Policies

Index Description for the Agent

Write the description so the agent knows when to query it: "Contains the complete HR policy handbook and employee handbook for all full-time employees,

including leave entitlements, benefits, expense policies, and conduct guidelines — current as of Q3 2025."

Product Documentation and User Manuals

Context Grounding in Action: Product Documentation & User Manuals

Product documentation and user manuals are among the highest-value candidates for Context Grounding indexing. Support teams, sales staff, and customers frequently need precise technical answers — and getting those answers wrong has direct business consequences. By indexing your product documentation, agents and Autopilot can deliver accurate, versionspecific answers instantly, without requiring a support ticket or a subject matter expert.

What Gets Indexed

  • Product feature descriptions and specifications
  • Step-by-step installation and setup guides
  • Configuration and integration instructions
  • Troubleshooting guides and known issue lists
  • Release notes and version changelogs
  • API references and developer guides
  • FAQs and how-to articles

How It Works in Practice

A support agent opens Autopilot and asks: "What are the system requirements for installing Product X version 3.2?" Autopilot queries the Product Documentation index, retrieves the exact requirements table from the v3.2 installation guide, and returns the answer with a citation to the specific manual section — no manual searching through PDFs required.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for direct technical lookups: "What ports does Product X require to be open on the firewall?" Use DeepRAG for complex multi-document questions: "How do I migrate from version 2.1 to version 3.2 while preserving custom configurations and ensuring compatibility with the current integration setup?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Use product name and version in the index name: ProductX-Docs-v3.2 or UserManuals-Hardware-2025 rather than just Docs or Manuals

Index Description for the Agent

Write it so the agent knows exactly when to query this index: "Contains product documentation, user manuals, installation guides, troubleshooting references, and release notes for Product X versions 2.x and 3.x. Use this index when users ask about product features, setup steps, configuration options, known issues, or version-specific behaviour."

Keeping the Index Current

Product documentation changes with every release. Best practice: Trigger an index sync automatically whenever a new version of the documentation is published to the Storage Bucket Use the Update Context Grounding Index activity in a release pipeline workflow Name new version indexes separately — ProductX-Docs-v4.0 — rather than overwriting the previous version, so historical queries remain accurate

Ingestion Mode Selection

Use Basic mode if manuals are primarily text-based PDFs or Word documents Use Advanced mode if manuals contain diagrams, scanned images, complex tables, or OCR-heavy content — common in hardware documentation and legacy manuals

Compliance and Regulatory Documents

Context Grounding in Action: Compliance & Regulatory Documents

Compliance and regulatory documents represent one of the most critical and risk-sensitive use cases for Context Grounding indexing. Employees and compliance officers frequently need precise, up-to-date answers about regulatory requirements — and an incorrect answer can result in legal penalties, audit failures, or reputational damage. By indexing compliance documents, agents and Autopilot deliver accurate, regulation-specific answers grounded in your organisation's actual compliance library.

What Gets Indexed

  • Industry regulations and regulatory frameworks (GDPR, HIPAA, SOX, Basel III)
  • Internal compliance policies and control procedures
  • Audit checklists and evidence requirements
  • Data privacy and data retention policies
  • Anti-money laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) procedures
  • Health and safety regulations and incident reporting procedures
  • Contractual compliance obligations and SLA terms

How It Works in Practice

A compliance officer asks: "What is our data retention period for customer transaction records under our current GDPR policy?" Autopilot queries the Compliance index, retrieves the exact retention schedule from the data privacy policy document, and returns the answer with a citation to the specific clause — giving the officer a reliable, auditable reference rather than a general interpretation.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for direct regulatory lookups: "What is the maximum fine under GDPR Article 83 for a Tier 2 violation?" Use DeepRAG for cross-regulation synthesis: "How do our GDPR data retention obligations interact with our SOX audit trail requirements when a customer requests deletion of their financial records?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Include the regulation name, scope, and date: Compliance-GDPR-2025, Compliance-AML-Procedures-Q2-2025 rather than just Compliance or Regulations Index Description for the Agent "Contains internal compliance policies, regulatory frameworks, audit procedures, and data privacy guidelines applicable to all business units. Covers GDPR, SOX, AML, and KYC requirements as of 2025. Use this index when users ask about regulatory obligations, compliance procedures, audit evidence requirements, or data handling rules." Keeping the Index Current — Critical for Compliance Regulatory documents change due to legislative updates, internal policy revisions, and audit findings. Unlike product documentation, outdated compliance content is not just inconvenient — it is a compliance risk in itself. Set a mandatory sync schedule aligned with regulatory review cycles — typically quarterly at minimum When a regulation is amended, trigger an immediate index sync using the Update Context Grounding Index activity

Maintain separate versioned indexes for each regulatory revision — Compliance-GDPR2024 and Compliance-GDPR-2025 — so the agent always references the currently applicable version Archive older indexes rather than deleting them, to support historical audit queries

Ingestion Mode Selection

Use Basic mode for text-based policy documents and internal procedure guides Use Advanced mode for scanned regulatory filings, complex formatted legal documents, and tables-heavy audit checklists that require OCR processing

Special Consideration: Threshold Setting

For compliance use cases, be especially cautious with the retrieval threshold setting. The UiPath documentation notes that setting threshold above zero can filter out relevant chunks and increase hallucinations. In a compliance context, a hallucinated answer — one not grounded in the actual regulation — carries significant risk. Keep the threshold at zero or test any non-zero value rigorously before production deployment. Ingestion Job Monitoring — Non-Negotiable for Compliance Subscribe to Context Grounding notifications for: Ingestion Job Failed — immediately alert the compliance team if a sync fails, since the index may be serving outdated content Ingestion Job Completed — confirm that the latest regulatory documents have been successfully indexed before communicating index availability to users

Technical Support Knowledge Bases

Context Grounding in Action: Technical Support Knowledge Bases

Technical support knowledge bases are a high-volume, high-frequency use case for Context Grounding indexing. Support agents handle dozens of queries daily — many of them repetitive — and the speed and accuracy of their answers directly impacts customer satisfaction. By indexing your support knowledge base, agents and Autopilot can instantly surface the right troubleshooting steps, known fixes, and escalation procedures, reducing average handle time and improving first-contact resolution rates.

What Gets Indexed

Troubleshooting guides and diagnostic flowcharts Known issue lists and workaround documentation

  • Error code references and resolution steps
  • Escalation procedures and routing guidelines
  • Product configuration guides for common support scenarios
  • Customer-reported bug logs and fix documentation
  • Internal support runbooks and standard operating procedures
  • SLA definitions for support tiers (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3)

How It Works in Practice

A support agent receives a call from a customer unable to connect to a VPN after a recent software update. The agent asks Autopilot: "What are the known VPN connectivity issues after the version 4.1 update and how are they resolved?" Autopilot queries the Technical Support Knowledge Base index, retrieves the relevant known issue entry with step-by-step resolution instructions, and returns the answer with a citation to the specific knowledge article — allowing the agent to resolve the issue in the first call without escalation.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for direct issue lookups: "What does error code E-4072 mean and how is it resolved?" Use DeepRAG for complex diagnostic questions: "A customer is experiencing intermittent login failures only on Chrome browsers after SSO was enabled and a proxy was configured — what combination of known issues and fixes applies to this scenario?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Include the product, support tier, and date: Support-KB-ProductX-Tier1-2025, Support-Runbooks-Infrastructure-Q3-2025 rather than just SupportDocs or KB Index Description for the Agent "Contains the technical support knowledge base including troubleshooting guides, known issue documentation, error code references, escalation procedures, and support runbooks for all products. Use this index when support agents ask about resolving customer issues, diagnosing errors, identifying known bugs, or following escalation procedures." Keeping the Index Current — Speed Is Critical Unlike compliance documents that change quarterly, support knowledge bases change continuously — new bugs are discovered, fixes are released, and workarounds are updated with every product release. Trigger an automatic index sync whenever a knowledge article is created or updated in the source system

Use the Update Context Grounding Index activity triggered by a Storage Bucket event or a scheduled daily sync When a known issue is resolved in a product release, update the knowledge article immediately and sync — agents must not continue citing outdated workarounds after a fix is available Maintain product-version-specific indexes where knowledge differs significantly between versions: Support-KB-ProductX-v3.x and Support-KB-ProductX-v4.x

Ingestion Mode Selection

Use Basic mode for text-based knowledge articles, runbooks, and procedure guides Use Advanced mode for knowledge bases that include screenshots, annotated diagrams, flowcharts, or scanned legacy documentation

Two-Index Architecture for Support

A practical design pattern for support use cases is to maintain two separate indexes and connect both to the support agent: Index 1 — Customer-Facing KB: Public-facing knowledge articles, user guides, and selfservice troubleshooting steps. Used when the agent needs to share a resolution with the customer directly. Index 2 — Internal Runbooks: Internal escalation procedures, backend system access steps, and confidential diagnostic procedures. Used when the agent needs internal guidance not suitable for sharing with the customer.

The agent's index descriptions make clear which index serves which purpose, and the agent selects the appropriate one based on whether the query requires a customer-facing or internal response.

Retrieval Tuning for High-Volume Support

Support knowledge bases tend to be large — hundreds or thousands of articles. Tuning is especially important: Number of results: Start with 5–8 retrieved chunks. Too few may miss the relevant article; too many increases token cost without improving accuracy. Threshold: Keep at zero. Support queries can be phrased in many ways by different agents, and a non-zero threshold risks filtering out the correct article when the query wording differs from the article's wording. Iterate using trace logs: If agents report that Autopilot is returning irrelevant articles, use the Trace view to inspect which chunks were retrieved and refine the index description or query approach accordingly.

  • Monitoring — Operational Necessity
  • For a live support operation, index failures have immediate operational impact:
  • Subscribe to Ingestion Job Failed notifications with immediate alerting to the support operations team Monitor the Trace view of agent runs to spot patterns of irrelevant retrievals — these indicate either index staleness or description misalignment Track which knowledge articles are most frequently retrieved — high-frequency retrievals on the same article may indicate a recurring product issue worth escalating to the product team

Legal Contracts and Agreements

Context Grounding in Action: Legal Contracts & Agreements (For Review Assistance) Legal contracts and agreements represent the most nuanced and high-stakes use case for Context Grounding indexing. Unlike HR policies or support knowledge bases where answers are relatively straightforward, contract review requires the ability to locate specific clauses, compare obligations across multiple documents, identify deviations from standard terms, and surface risks — all with precise citations. Context Grounding enables legal teams, procurement officers, and contract managers to interact with their entire contract library through natural language, dramatically reducing the time spent manually searching through lengthy legal documents.

What Gets Indexed

  • Master Service Agreements (MSAs) and framework contracts
  • Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) and confidentiality clauses
  • Vendor and supplier contracts with obligation schedules
  • Statement of Work (SOW) documents and deliverable definitions
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA) terms embedded in contracts
  • Renewal and termination clauses across the contract portfolio
  • Standard contract templates and clause libraries
  • Regulatory and compliance obligations embedded in contracts
  • Amendment and addendum documents linked to parent contracts

How It Works in Practice

A procurement officer is reviewing a new vendor contract and needs to check whether its liability cap aligns with the organisation's standard terms. They ask Autopilot: "What is the liability cap in our standard MSA template, and how does it compare to what vendor contracts typically include?" Autopilot queries the Legal Contracts index, retrieves the liability clause from the standard MSA template and relevant clauses from existing vendor contracts, and returns a comparative summary with citations to each source document — enabling the officer to immediately identify whether the new contract deviates from standard terms.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for locating specific clauses: "What is the payment terms clause in the Vendor ABC contract?" or "What are the termination for convenience conditions in our standard MSA?" Use DeepRAG for complex cross-contract analysis: "Across all active vendor contracts, which ones have a liability cap below £500,000, include auto-renewal clauses, and do not have a data processing addendum — and what are the renewal dates for each?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Organise by contract type, counterparty category, and year: Legal-MSA-Templates-2025, Legal-Vendor-Contracts-Active-2025, Legal-NDA-Library2025 rather than a single flat Contracts index

Why Separate Indexes Matter for Legal

A single large contracts index creates retrieval ambiguity — the agent cannot distinguish between a template clause and an actual executed contract clause. Separate indexes by contract type and status: Index 1 — Standard Templates: MSA templates, NDA templates, SOW templates. Used when checking what the standard terms should be. Index 2 — Active Executed Contracts: Currently live vendor and customer contracts. Used when checking what was actually agreed. Index 3 — Expired/Archived Contracts: Historical contracts for reference and dispute resolution. Kept separate to avoid polluting active contract queries.

Index Description for the Agent

"Contains the organisation's legal contract library including standard MSA, NDA, and SOW templates, active executed vendor and customer contracts, and associated amendments. Use this index when users need to locate specific contract clauses, check standard terms, identify renewal or termination dates, or compare obligations across contracts. Always cite the specific contract name, section number, and clause when returning results."

Critical Configuration: Always Cite Sources

For legal use cases, citations are not optional — they are essential. The agent's system prompt must explicitly instruct it to always cite the document name, section, and clause number for

every answer it provides. An uncited legal answer is unusable in practice because the reviewing party must be able to verify the source independently. Include this instruction in the agent or Autopilot system prompt: "When answering questions about contracts, always provide the full citation: contract name, section number, and clause title. Never paraphrase legal obligations without citing the exact source clause."

Ingestion Mode Selection

Use Basic mode for digitally created contracts in PDF or Word format where text is clean and selectable Use Advanced mode for scanned legacy contracts, contracts with complex formatting, signature pages with embedded images, or documents containing tables of obligations and schedules

  • Keeping the Index Current — Contract Lifecycle Awareness
  • Contracts have a lifecycle — they are executed, amended, renewed, and terminated. The index must reflect the current state of each contract at all times:
  • When a contract is executed, add it to the Active Executed Contracts index and trigger a sync When a contract amendment is signed, add the amendment document and re-sync — the agent must be aware of both the original terms and the amendment When a contract expires or is terminated, move it from the Active index to the Archived index and re-sync both — agents must not cite expired terms as current obligations When a renewal changes terms, treat it as a new document version and index accordingly

Use the Update Context Grounding Index activity in a contract lifecycle workflow triggered by status changes in your contract management system. Threshold Setting — Maximum Caution Legal contract review has zero tolerance for hallucination. A fabricated clause or an incorrectly retrieved obligation could lead to incorrect legal decisions. Keep threshold at zero to ensure no potentially relevant clause is filtered out Use DeepRAG with citations rather than Semantic Search for any query that will be used to make a legal or commercial decision Always instruct users to verify cited clauses directly in the source document before acting on any agent-provided answer — Autopilot assists review, it does not replace it Include a disclaimer in the agent's system prompt: "Always verify contract clauses in the original executed document before making legal or commercial decisions. This Autopilot assists review and does not constitute legal advice."

Retrieval Tuning for Large Contract Libraries

Large organisations may have thousands of contracts. Tuning is critical: Number of results: Use a higher number (8–10) for contract review queries since relevant clauses may appear across multiple documents Index descriptions: Be highly specific about what each index contains — active vs archived, templates vs executed, vendor vs customer Iterate with trace logs: If the agent retrieves clauses from expired contracts when querying active ones, the index separation or descriptions need refinement

Monitoring

Subscribe to Ingestion Job Failed notifications — a failed sync after a contract amendment means the agent is serving outdated terms, which is a material risk Use the Trace view to verify that citation sources are correct — spot-check that the document and section cited by the agent match the actual contract Monitor query patterns — frequent queries about the same contract may indicate an upcoming renewal or dispute that the legal team should be aware of

Internal Process Guides and SOPs

Context Grounding in Action: Internal Process Guides & Standard Operating Procedures

(SOPs)

Internal process guides and Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) are the operational backbone of any organisation. They define exactly how work must be performed — step by step, role by role, system by system. Yet in most organisations, SOPs are stored in shared drives, rarely read, and frequently ignored simply because they are hard to find and harder to navigate. Context Grounding transforms this by making your entire SOP library instantly queryable through natural language — so any employee can ask exactly what they need to know about a process and receive precise, step-by-step guidance from the authoritative source document, with a citation proving where the answer came from.

What Gets Indexed

  • Department-level SOPs for Finance, HR, IT, Operations, and Procurement
  • Process guides for onboarding, offboarding, and role transitions
  • System usage guides for ERP, CRM, and line-of-business applications
  • Quality management procedures and inspection checklists
  • Change management and change request procedures
  • Incident response and business continuity procedures
  • Health and safety SOPs and emergency response guides
  • Audit preparation procedures and evidence collection guides
  • Approval workflows and authority delegation matrices

How It Works in Practice

A new employee in the Finance department needs to process a vendor invoice for the first time. Instead of asking a colleague or hunting through a shared drive, they ask Autopilot: "What are the steps to process a vendor invoice in the Finance department?" Autopilot queries the Internal Process Guides index, retrieves the exact invoice processing SOP, and returns a clear step-bystep response with a citation to the specific SOP document and section — giving the new employee an authoritative answer in seconds without interrupting anyone else's work.

Which Retrieval Method to Use

Use Semantic Search for locating a specific procedure: "What is the approval process for raising a purchase order above £10,000?" or "What steps do I follow to onboard a new contractor?" Use DeepRAG for cross-process questions: "If a vendor invoice arrives without a matching purchase order and the supplier is flagged for a compliance review, which SOPs apply, in what sequence should they be followed, and who needs to be notified at each stage?"

Index Naming Best Practice

Organise by department, process category, and version: SOPs-Finance-AP-2025, SOPs-IT-ChangeManagement-v3, ProcessGuides-HR-Onboarding2025 rather than a single flat SOPs or Processes index Why Separate Indexes by Department and Function A single monolithic SOP index creates two problems. First, retrieval ambiguity — when a Finance user asks about an approval process, the agent may retrieve IT change approval SOPs instead of Finance purchase approval SOPs. Second, access control — some SOPs contain sensitive operational details (incident response procedures, security protocols) that should not be surfaced to all employees. Recommended index architecture: Index 1 — General Employee SOPs: Processes applicable to all staff — expense claims, leave requests, IT access requests, building access. Connected to the general Autopilot for Everyone. Index 2 — Department-Specific SOPs: Finance, HR, IT, Operations procedures. Connected to the relevant Specialized Autopilot for each department. Index 3 — Sensitive/Restricted SOPs: Incident response, business continuity, security procedures. Connected only to agents used by authorised roles.

Index Description for the Agent

"Contains internal Standard Operating Procedures and process guides for all operational departments including Finance, HR, IT, and Operations. Covers invoice processing, procurement approvals, onboarding and offboarding, system access requests, change management, and quality procedures as of 2025. Use this index when employees ask how to perform a specific task, follow an approval process, or understand the steps in an operational procedure."

Connecting SOPs to the Right Specialized Autopilot

SOPs are most powerful when connected to a role-specific Specialized Autopilot rather than the general Autopilot for Everyone: Finance Autopilot → connected to Finance SOPs index → answers questions about AP/AR procedures, month-end close steps, expense approval workflows IT Support Autopilot → connected to IT SOPs index → answers questions about change request procedures, incident escalation steps, access provisioning HR Autopilot → connected to HR SOPs index → answers questions about onboarding checklists, offboarding procedures, performance review processes

This ensures that when a Finance employee asks a process question, they receive Financespecific guidance — not a generic or cross-departmental answer. Keeping the Index Current — Version Control Is Critical SOPs are living documents. They are updated when systems change, regulations evolve, organisational structures shift, or audit findings identify gaps. An outdated SOP in the index is not just unhelpful — it actively guides employees to follow the wrong procedure. Assign a SOP owner for each document who is responsible for triggering an index sync when the SOP is updated Use a versioned naming convention in both the document and the index: InvoiceProcessing-SOP-v4.2 not just Invoice-Processing-SOP When a SOP is retired or replaced, remove it from the index immediately and sync — the agent must never cite a superseded procedure Set a mandatory review cycle — typically annual — and trigger an index sync after each review even if no changes were made, to confirm the indexed content matches the current document Use the Update Context Grounding Index activity triggered by a document management system event whenever a SOP document is updated or republished

Ingestion Mode Selection

Use Basic mode for text-based SOPs in Word or PDF format with clear paragraph structure Use Advanced mode for SOPs that include process flowcharts, decision trees, annotated screenshots of system interfaces, or scanned legacy procedure documents

Special Consideration: Step-by-Step Structure

SOPs are inherently sequential — Step 1, Step 2, Step 3. When ingesting SOPs, the chunking process may split a multi-step procedure across different chunks, causing the agent to retrieve only part of a procedure. To mitigate this: Structure SOPs with clear numbered headings for each step so chunking boundaries align with logical procedure breaks Use DeepRAG rather than Semantic Search when the user's question requires the full endto-end procedure rather than a single step In the agent's system prompt, instruct it to retrieve the complete procedure when a user asks "how to" questions: "When a user asks how to perform a process, always retrieve and present the complete step-by-step procedure, not just individual steps."

Threshold Setting

Keep threshold at zero for SOP queries — process steps are often described in operational language that may differ from how employees phrase their questions, and a non-zero threshold risks filtering out the correct procedure Use trace logs to monitor whether retrieved chunks are capturing complete procedures or only partial steps, and adjust the number of results accordingly

Monitoring

Subscribe to Ingestion Job Failed notifications — a failed SOP sync means employees may be following outdated procedures, which is an operational and potentially a safety risk Use the Trace view to verify that when an employee asks a process question, the correct SOP version is being retrieved — not a superseded version Monitor query frequency by SOP topic — high-frequency queries on the same procedure may indicate that the SOP is unclear and needs to be rewritten for better comprehension, or that employees are not aware the procedure exists Ingestion Job Completed notifications confirm that the latest SOP versions have been successfully indexed and are ready for employee queries

Creating & Maintaining Knowledge Indexes

Building a reliable knowledge index starts with creating a new index in Orchestrator, connecting it to your document sources, and choosing the right configuration. It’s essential to use clear, versioned names and keep indexes in sync with document updates to ensure accuracy. Regular maintenance—such as scheduling syncs or triggering updates when documents change—keeps your AI answers current and trustworthy.

Introduction to Creating and Syncing an Index in Orchestrator

This guide will walk you through the process of creating and syncing an index in Orchestrator, ensuring your organization's documentation is always up to date and easily accessible.

  1. 1
    Access the Indexes Page
    Navigate to the Orchestrator and go to the Indexes page.
  2. 2
    Create a New Index
    Select Create Index and provide the required details such as the index name, description, and source configuration.
  3. 3
    Connect to a Data Source
    Connect the index to a Storage Bucket or another data source that contains your documents.
  4. 4
    Select Ingestion Mode
    Choose the ingestion mode that suits your needs: Basic or Advanced.
  5. 5
    Set Up Syncing
    Schedule or trigger regular syncs to ensure the index remains current with the latest documentation.

Summary: Keeping Your Index Accurate

Retrieval Methods: Semantic Search vs DeepRAG

Retrieving the right information from your knowledge index is just as important as building it. UiPath offers two primary retrieval methods: Semantic Search and DeepRAG. Each has its own strengths and is suited to different types of queries. Understanding when to use each method ensures your AI agents deliver precise, relevant answers—whether the question is simple or requires synthesizing information from multiple sources.

Compare the Two Retrieval Methods for Knowledge Search

Semantic SearchDeepRAGComparison Table

Semantic Search uses embeddings and cosine similarity search to find information based on the meaning and intent of a query, rather than exact keyword matches. It retrieves the most relevant content from indexed business data by understanding the contextual relationship between the user's question and the stored information. Semantic Search is ideal for natural language queries where users may phrase questions differently from the source content. It enables AI agents to locate relevant knowledge across documents and provide accurate, context-aware responses.

Example: "What is our return policy?" → The system identifies and retrieves the document sections that are semantically related to return policies, even if the exact phrase "return policy" does not appear in the document.

DeepRAG is an advanced retrieval method that combines retrieval and reasoning to answer complex questions. Unlike Semantic Search, which returns the most relevant content chunks, DeepRAG can analyze and synthesize information from multiple documents and sources to generate a comprehensive response. DeepRAG performs additional processing to improve answer quality, making it suitable for scenarios that require deeper understanding, multi-step reasoning, or combining information from different parts of the knowledge base. It also provides citations and references to the source content used in generating the response.
